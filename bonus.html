Bonus Scene · Amodini Raghav × Sree · Domestic

Morning After

This was written because someone in the comments asked what happens the morning after the rain scene. Here's your answer.

She found him in the kitchen before sunrise.

Not because she was looking. Just because the house was quiet and that was where her feet took her. The kind of quiet that only existed between 5 and 6 in the morning, when the city hadn't decided to wake up yet and everything felt like it was still thinking about whether to bother.

He didn't say anything at first.

He just reached for a second cup from the shelf without turning around, and slid it across the counter in her direction. Like he'd heard her come in. Like he'd been expecting her, maybe, or at least not surprised.

She wrapped both hands around the cup. Still warm. He'd made enough for two.

"You've been up a while," she said.

"Couldn't sleep."

"Me neither."

They stood there. The sky outside was doing the thing it does just before dawn where it can't quite decide what color it wants to be. Some dark blue still. Some grey starting at the edges. A faint suggestion of something warmer coming.

She didn't ask what he'd been thinking about. He didn't explain. That was one of the things she was starting to understand about Raghav. The silences weren't empty. They were just his version of saying something.

She drank the chai. He drank his. The kitchen held them both without asking either of them to explain why they were there.

Later, when the house started to wake up, when Idhayan's particular brand of chaos began its morning performance down the hall, Raghav put his cup in the sink and turned around. He looked at her the way he sometimes did when he thought she wasn't watching. Like he was making note of something. Like he was filing it away somewhere he planned to keep it.

She pretended not to notice.

She was getting good at that too.

Bonus Scene · Amodini Nisarg × Aadhya · Quiet Tension

The Argument That Wasn't

For everyone who asked about Nisarg and Aadhya having a fight. Here's the thing. They don't fight the way you think they do.

It wasn't even a real fight.

That was the thing neither of them said out loud. Aadhya had said three sentences. Nisarg had said two. And then the room had gone so quiet that Idhayan had looked up from his blocks, looked between them with the specific attention of a two year old who has learned that silence from adults means something has shifted, and then gone back to his blocks. He stacked one block on top of another. The tower fell. He didn't look up again.

Nisarg walked to the window.

Aadhya didn't follow. She wasn't going to follow. That was the whole point.

The thing about Nisarg was that he communicated in precision. He said exactly what he meant and he meant exactly what he said, and right now what he'd said was nothing, and that was the part she was sitting with. The nothing. Which in Nisarg's language was actually quite a lot.

She picked up Idhayan's fallen block. Handed it back to him. He accepted it with the gravity of someone receiving a very important document.

"I'm not angry," Nisarg said, still looking out the window.

"I know."

"I just need a minute."

"I know that too."

She didn't tell him to take his time. She didn't tell him it was fine. It wasn't a performance of patience. It was just patience. The real kind, that doesn't need an audience.

Idhayan's tower fell again. This time he laughed.

The room breathed a little easier.

That was the whole argument. Beginning, middle, and the kind of ending that doesn't announce itself. Just happens. Just settles.

Bonus Scene · Amodini Yug × Devanshi · 3AM

Tell Me Something Else

Set after everything. When the worst has already happened and the only thing left to do is be awake together at 3AM.

"You're supposed to be asleep," he said.

"So are you."

Yug pulled the blanket tighter around her shoulders. From behind. She didn't lean into it, not right away. But she didn't move away either, which was its own kind of answer.

"I keep thinking about it," she said.

"I know."

"I don't want to keep thinking about it."

"I know that too."

The city outside was doing its 3AM thing. Barely there. Just enough ambient noise to remind you that the world was still going, that other people existed, that time was still moving in the regular direction.

He rested his chin on top of her head. She let him.

"Tell me something else," she said. "Anything. Something that has nothing to do with any of this."

He thought about it. She could feel him actually thinking, which was rare for Yug, who usually just talked. The pause meant he was taking her seriously. That mattered more than she expected it to.

"Idhayan called me baba yesterday," he said. "By accident. He didn't mean to. He was looking for Raghav bhai and he just. Said it."

She laughed.

Just barely. Just a small exhale that was more surprised than anything. But it was real and it was hers and the room felt different after it.

"What did you do?" she asked.

"I said 'yes' and handed him the biscuit he wanted."

"You didn't correct him."

"He was very committed to the biscuit. It felt like the wrong moment."

She laughed again. Properly this time.

He held on a little tighter. She let him do that too.

Outside, the city kept going. Inside, 3AM felt a little less like 3AM and a little more like the particular quiet that comes just before something settles.

Character POV · Amodini Idhayan · The World According to Him

Everything Is Fine Actually

I wrote this for myself, honestly. I needed to know what the house looked like from two feet off the ground.

The big people do a lot of talking.

This is something he has noticed. They talk in the morning and they talk at meals and sometimes they talk very quietly in the kitchen late at night when they think nobody is awake. He is sometimes awake. He doesn't tell them this because he has learned that being awake after a certain time results in being put back in the bed, and the bed is less interesting than the kitchen at night.

He knows certain things with complete certainty. The woman with the soft voice who smells like flowers is his. The tall quiet one who makes him chai in a tiny cup is also his. Yug mama is his favourite, which he makes clear by demanding to be carried by him specifically and rejecting all substitutes.

There is a new person now. She came and then she stayed, which is the best thing a person can do. She put lights on all the windows. He helped. He held one diya very carefully with both hands and walked very slowly and nobody told him he was doing it wrong, even though he was probably doing it at least a little wrong.

The house smells like food and something warm. He doesn't have a word for the second thing yet. He will learn it eventually. For now he just knows that when he wakes up from his nap and the smell is there, everything is fine.

He walks to the kitchen. Yug mama is there. This was predicted.

"Baba," he says, by accident.

Yug mama's face does something he doesn't have words for either. Then he holds out the biscuit.

He takes it. He knew he would.

Everything is fine actually.

Deleted Scene · Chapter 3 Cut Raghav · What Didn't Make It

What He Almost Said

This was cut because it revealed too much of Raghav's interiority too early. The story needed to keep him opaque for longer. But I kept it because it's true to him. Everything he was already feeling by Chapter 3, before he'd admitted any of it.

He stood in the hallway for longer than he needed to.

The house was quiet. Sree had gone to her room an hour ago. Everyone else was asleep. There was no reason to be standing in the hallway at 11pm looking at a closed door.

He was very aware of this and did it anyway.

The thing was he had almost said something. At dinner. When she'd made that comment about the architecture of old houses, about how they were designed to hold families rather than just people, and he'd looked up and she'd been looking at the ceiling the way she looked at things she was genuinely thinking about, not performing interest in, and he'd thought something very specific and then said nothing.

He was good at that. Saying nothing.

He walked back to his room. Got into bed. Stared at the ceiling for exactly the amount of time it took him to decide, conclusively, that he was not thinking about her.

He was thinking about the architecture of old houses. The way they were designed to hold families rather than just people.

That was all.

That was definitely all.

Festival Special · Amodini The Whole Family · Diwali

The Year They Did Diwali Properly

Happy Diwali to everyone who reads these. This one is for all of you.

The house smelled like marigold and ghee and something frying in oil in the kitchen that nobody had officially announced but everyone could smell from three rooms away.

Devanshi had put diyas on every window ledge.

Nobody told her to. She just did it, the evening before, quietly, taking each clay lamp from the bag she'd bought herself and setting it down with the kind of care that meant she'd thought about exactly where each one would look best. Yug had watched her do the last window from the doorway without saying anything. She hadn't noticed him. Or she had and decided not to mention it. With Devanshi it was sometimes hard to tell.

Idhayan was wearing something orange and was very upset about it. He had made his position on the orange kurta extremely clear. He was overruled.

Aadhya lit the first diya. Nisarg stood next to her close enough that their hands were almost touching, which for Nisarg was basically a declaration. Raghav stood at the edge of the courtyard and watched his family and looked like a man who has realised, very recently, that this is what all that responsibility was for.

Sree stood next to him.

They didn't say anything. They just watched the lights go on, one by one, until the whole house was glowing.

"It looks different this year," she said eventually.

"It is different this year," he said.

Idhayan ran past them wearing the orange kurta and screaming with joy. The diya in his hand was very slightly lopsided.

Nobody corrected it.

The house held all of them. It was built for exactly this.

Letter · Unsent · Amodini Sree · To Nobody in Particular

The Letter She Never Sent

Sree wrote this somewhere around Chapter 9. She didn't send it. It's not addressed to anyone specific. I found it in her drafts.

I don't know what I'm doing here.

That's the honest version. The more composed version, the one I give when people ask, involves words like "settling in" and "adjusting" and "it's going well." Those things are also true. But this is the letter nobody's reading so I can say the other thing too.

I came here for reasons that made sense at the time. I'm still here for reasons I haven't fully worked out yet.

There's a man in this house who makes chai at 5am and never explains why. There's a toddler who has decided I'm his person and doesn't ask permission. There's a family that argues about which film to watch and somehow manages to feel like the most solid thing I've stood near in years.

I don't know what to do with any of this.

I grew up in a house where you learned not to take up too much space. I'm learning, here, that this house has different rules. That there's enough room. That people here actually mean it when they ask how you are.

I still don't know what I'm doing here.

But I think I might stay long enough to find out.

That feels like enough, for now.

— S
